# AI1 CLI

**Author:** Nik Bear Brown

---

## One-Sentence Pitch

You learn to make books by talking to a CLI agent — starting with a finished, fact-checked textbook you download and change, and ending with one you built from scratch and exported to Kindle and Canvas and Medhavy (.mdx files rather than md).

## The Argument

Most books about AI tools explain; this book *assigns*. Its claim is that the AI+1 system — one human plus AI agents, with the human as the load-bearing author — is learned the way the system itself works: by doing whole tasks with real artifacts, behind real gates, leaving a real trail. So every chapter is a short overview followed mostly by exercises, and every exercise produces something you can open: an EPUB, a flashcard deck, a Canvas import, a signed gate.

The deeper argument is about where the human belongs. AI made drafting, formatting, and converting cheap. What it did not make cheap is judgment: deciding what a book is for, whether a claim is true, whether a rewrite sounds like you. The exercises are sequenced so the reader *experiences* that boundary rather than being told about it — generation is the fallback, the signature is human, and the first thing you ever do is change someone else's finished book and watch the whole factory rebuild around your one sentence.

## The Gap

Pandoc manuals teach conversion; prompt-engineering books teach prompting; self-publishing guides teach platforms. Nothing teaches the *system*: an agent-operated repository where a constitution (AI1.md), a phase spine (CONDUCTOR.md), a fact dictionary, and verified-artifact gates turn talking into a publishable, fact-checked book. This book is the manual for that system, written as exercises — and it is itself produced by the system it teaches.

## The Reader

A non-technical author, teacher, or student who can use email and chat with an AI — nothing more. They may have a subject they know deeply and no path to a book; or a course that needs a text, quizzes, and a Canvas shell they can't afford to buy. After this book they can: download and rebuild an existing AI+1 book; re-voice, re-title, and re-cover it; generate its study layer; export to Kindle and Canvas; record and sign facts; and take an idea from Blueprint to a finished, exported book of their own.

## High-Level Outline

**Part I — Make a Book Yours (download → change → ship)**
Start from a finished, fact-checked book (e.g., the cancer-biology textbook). Change one sentence and rebuild. Then retitle it, re-voice it, generate quizzes and flashcards, export to Kindle and Canvas, and learn the fact dictionary and sidecar sign-offs along the way. Whole tasks from minute one; the system's rules are encountered, not lectured.

**Part II — Change the Substance**
Move from editing to authoring inside the downloaded book: swap in a chapter on your own subject, practice the human rewrite (the gate no agent may sign), and make and audit figures. The reader crosses from "operating the factory" to "being the author the factory serves."

**Part III — Build From Scratch**
Blueprint a new book (Phase 0), research before drafting, draft and rewrite behind gates, fact-check, illustrate, and ship to every target. Part III is the seven-phase spine walked end to end — the reader's own honest run.

## Out of Scope

- **What Medhavy does.** This book teaches producing the `.mdx` export; Medhavy's capabilities (adaptive practice, study paths, Ask-AI — the things Kindle and Canvas can't do) are a separate book, cross-referenced at Chapter 12. Most readers know Kindle and Canvas; nobody should need a second book to finish this one.
