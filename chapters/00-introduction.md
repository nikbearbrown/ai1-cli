# Introduction

You are holding a book that was made by the system it teaches. Every chapter you're about to read was planned behind a gate, drafted by an AI agent, rewritten by a human who signed it, fact-checked against recorded sources, and built into the file you're reading by a script anyone can run. That isn't a boast. It's the argument.

**AI+1** is one human plus AI agents, making books neither could make alone. The AI does what AI is good at: research, drafting, format conversion, figure generation, building outputs. The **+1** — you — does what cannot be delegated: deciding what the book *is*, rewriting it until it sounds like you, judging whether a claim is true, and signing your name to every decision that matters. The "+1" is not a reviewer bolted onto an AI pipeline. The +1 is the reason the output is a book and not a pile of fluent text.

Here is the problem this book exists to solve. AI made producing book-shaped text nearly free — and the result is a flood of fluent, unchecked, unowned books that nobody stands behind. The scarce thing was never typing. It was judgment: what to include, what to cut, what to verify, what to claim. AI+1 is a production system built around that scarcity. The agent executes; gates stop the line at every decision that needs a human; everything leaves a trail. You will not learn this by being told. You will learn it by shipping a book.

## What you'll actually do

One project runs through all twelve chapters. You'll download the *Writing Guide* — a finished, fact-checked, 24-chapter composition textbook — and remake it into a **copywriting book**. Everyone writes; no special expertise required to play. Along the way you will: research and sign a plan (Chapter 1), rebuild and re-title a real book (2), rewrite a chapter in another voice and judge the result (3), generate quizzes and flashcards and catch what the generator got wrong (4), export to Canvas (5), record and sign facts (6), cut chapters with logged reasons (7), convert what survives and sign your first author's gate (8), write the chapters copywriting needs that composition never had (9), hold the manuscript accountable to the plan you signed (10), fact-check everything (11), and ship to Kindle, Canvas, and Medhavy (12). A short coda shows you the same loop from an empty starter — your own book, next.

## The three ways in

You don't have to start from scratch, and you don't have to start here.

1. **Download a finished book.** Clone a complete, fact-checked AI+1 book from GitHub and make it yours — rewrite, re-voice, rebuild. (This book's running project is exactly that path.)
2. **Learn by doing.** Open this repo in your CLI agent and type `help`. Build the EPUB, make a flashcard deck, rewrite one chapter in another voice — small, safe exercises before you commit to a book of your own.
3. **Build from scratch.** Run **Blueprint** — the planning consultant you'll meet in Chapter 1 — on your own idea, then follow the seven-phase spine.

## How to read this book

Each chapter is a short overview — just enough to know what you're doing and why the system works that way — followed by exercises. **The exercises are the chapter.** Every one ends with an artifact you can open (an EPUB, a deck, a report, a signed gate) and a line in the trail. If you read without doing, you'll finish with opinions; if you do the exercises, you'll finish with a book.

Two habits start now and never stop. First: *the agent does the typing.* You will not edit config files or memorize commands — you'll tell your agent what you want, and it will run the scripts and report back. If you can use email and chat with an AI, you have every prerequisite this book assumes. Second: *nothing is true because the AI said it.* Claims trace to recorded sources; artifacts carry sign-off records; gates don't open until a human closes them. You'll feel the difference between fluent and verified by Chapter 4 at the latest, when a perfectly confident quiz question turns out to be testing the wrong thing.

**Setup (one box, once):** install a CLI agent — Cowork, Claude Code, or Codex — sign in, and open this repository's folder. Type `help`. The agent will ask whether you want to continue with this book or do warm-up exercises first. Everything else, it handles.

The plan comes before the prose. Turn the page and sign your first one.
