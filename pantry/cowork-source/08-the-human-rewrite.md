# Chapter 8 — The Human Rewrite: The Seam

*The pipeline cannot enforce this gate. The author either does this work or the book is a Cowork dump with figures.*

---

Read both paragraphs. Then keep reading.

> **Version A.**
> Domain research is one of the most important steps in any textbook writing project. Without good research, your book cannot succeed. In today's rapidly evolving design landscape, understanding what AI is doing to your profession is essential. Studies have shown that 78% of design firms are integrating AI tools into their workflows. This means designers must adapt quickly.

> **Version B.**
> Three LLMs answering the same question about your profession give you something no single LLM does: a map of agreement, divergence, and silence. Claude knows the published research. ChatGPT has read the trade press. Gemini has scraped the LinkedIn posts. Run the same prompt across all three on a Tuesday afternoon, and by Wednesday morning you have a domain research brief — the document Blueprint will demand at /i1.

---

Both versions open the same chapter. Both are 75 words. One is the Cowork draft. The other is the author's rewrite. You knew which was which by the second sentence.

Version A opens in summary; Version B opens in a scene. Version A's specificity is fabricated — the 78% has no source behind it; Version B's specificity is operational — three named tools, a real Tuesday, a real command. Version A could be about marketing, education, or small business. Version B could only be about a designer drafting their own AI+1 textbook. Version A ends gesturing; Version B ends pointing.

Naming what you just noticed is the easy part. Producing Version B from Version A — for one chapter, then another, then twelve more — is the work this chapter teaches. It is also the work the model cannot do.

The reason the model cannot produce Version B is what James Baldwin named in 1962: "The artist is distinguished from all other responsible actors in society... by the fact that he is his own test-tube, his own laboratory."[^baldwin] The voice in Version B was paid for. Ten years of design practice, a thousand brand-pitch Tuesdays, the muscle memory of explaining a research process to a client over Wednesday-morning coffee — that is the laboratory. The model has not been to that laboratory. It can produce text *about* the laboratory. It cannot produce text *from* it.

This chapter is the seam where the laboratory enters the book.

[^baldwin]: Baldwin, James (1962). "The Creative Process." In *Creative America*. Reprinted in *The Price of the Ticket* (1985). St. Martin's.

## Why this chapter is different from every other chapter

Every other chapter in this book describes a step the pipeline enforces. Skip Chapter 5 and there is no scaffold. Skip Chapter 6 and the Chapter Writer refuses. Skip Chapter 7 and there is nothing to look at.

You can skip Chapter 8.

Nothing in the pipeline stops you. You can run the Chapter Writer in Chapter 7, accept whatever it produces, run the Finishing Pass in Chapter 9 over Cowork's prose, add the LLM enrichment in Chapter 10, and submit to KDP in Chapter 12. The build script will produce an EPUB. Kindle will accept it. You will have a book on sale within a week.

What you will not have is your book. You will have a Cowork dump with figures, indistinguishable in voice from any other Cowork dump produced anywhere else in your field this month. The fluency trap returns — this time not in a chapter your reader might catch, but in the entire artifact your name is on. The book you produce by skipping this chapter is exactly the kind of generic output Chapter 1 taught you to recognize in someone else's work.

There is a seduction to skipping that is worth naming honestly. The Cowork draft is good enough to feel finished. The fake percentage is in there, and you know it is fake, but the sentence reads fluently and removing it feels like an aesthetic decision rather than an ethical one. The domain examples are from marketing and education rather than graphic design, but the chapter hangs together and the reader might not notice. These are the tells of the fluency trap at book scale — the draft is attractive enough to stop your judgment too early. The specific mechanism is now familiar; the chapter asks only that you apply it one level up.

Doug Lemov's observation in *Practice Perfect* is the diagnosis: the danger is "polishing what is easy to polish rather than what most needs polishing."[^lemov] A Cowork draft can pass a checklist of surface improvements and still be wrong in the deeper way — still be not yours, still be the model's version of what a chapter about your practice sounds like. This chapter teaches the test as a scaffold the writer eventually outgrows, not as a scorecard to hand back to the model.

[^lemov]: Lemov, Doug, Erica Woolway, and Katie Yezzi (2012). *Practice Perfect: 42 Rules for Getting Better at Getting Better*. Jossey-Bass.

## Five failure modes as rewrite targets

Chapter 7 named the five Cowork failure modes diagnostically — here is what went wrong. This chapter names them prescriptively — here is what to do about each one.

**Voice drift → specificity injection.**

The Cowork version: *"In today's rapidly evolving design landscape, AI tools are reshaping how creative professionals approach their work."*

The move: cut the generality, replace with a specific moment. *"It is 2:47 p.m. on a Wednesday. The client just shared a Midjourney image and asked if you can use it for the homepage."* Joseph Williams's principle from *Style* is the technical mechanism: lead with old information the reader can anchor on — the designer, the client, the Wednesday afternoon — then introduce new information.[^williams] The specific defeats the generic at the sentence level, and the sentence level is where voice either lives or dies.

[^williams]: Williams, Joseph M. and Joseph Bizup (2014). *Style: Lessons in Clarity and Grace* (11th ed.). Pearson.

**Fabricated specificity → cite, replace, or cut.**

The Cowork version: *"Studies show 78% of design firms are integrating AI tools into their workflows."*

Three responses, in order of preference. First: find the number in the pantry; if it is there and real, cite the source by title and date. Second: replace with a number you can defend — *"I count five major design tools that shipped generative features in 2024: Adobe Firefly, Figma AI, Canva Magic Studio, Photoshop's Generative Fill, and Midjourney's Style Reference."* Third: cut the sentence. Fabricated specificity that cannot be cited or replaced is decoration. The chapter is better without it than with a number no one can find.

**Missing domain judgment → one judgment per page.**

The Cowork version: *"Designers must consider the implications of using AI tools in their workflows."*

The move: replace the sentence with a judgment only you can make. *"I do not let Midjourney near a brand-identity exploration before the strategy phase is signed off. Generative tools widen the visual field too early and confuse stakeholders into thinking we are designing when we are still listening."* This is not an opinion about AI in general. It is a judgment about your practice in particular. Baldwin's test-tube made operational: one judgment per page, minimum, that could not have been written by anyone who has not lived the practice.

**Padded middle → cut 25%, restructure remainder.**

The Cowork version: a 5,000-word chapter whose argument fits in 3,500 words.

The move: read pages two through four with a pencil. Cross out any sentence that does not advance a claim, deliver an example, or name a trade-off. Read what remains. Does it still hold together? If yes, the cuts are real improvements. If no, the cuts have revealed a structural problem the padding was hiding — the problem is in the architecture, not the word count. Nancy Sommers's 1980 finding is what makes this move efficient: experienced writers revise at the level of argument and paragraph, not at the level of words.[^sommers] Cut 25% of words and the argument's actual shape shows up.

[^sommers]: Sommers, Nancy (1980). "Revision Strategies of Student Writers and Experienced Adult Writers." *College Composition and Communication*, 31(4), 378–388.

**Bridge questions that don't bridge → name the next chapter's deliverable.**

The Cowork version: *"What does this mean for the future of design?"*

The move: open the next chapter's spec. Find what it actually delivers. Rewrite the bridge to name that thing. *"Three LLMs is a research move. Blueprint is the instructional architecture that turns the research into a book spec. Chapter 4 walks you through the session that produces your Blueprint.md — the two hours that make the next four weeks possible."* The bridge is a commitment, not a gesture. A question about the future of design is a topic heading shaped like a question. A commitment names where the answer arrives.

A sixth rewrite target, off the official list: *sycophancy reversal*. Cowork drafts agree too easily with whatever the Blueprint.md framed.[^sycophancy] When you rewrite, look for places the draft accepts a claim without testing it. Pushing back at one point per chapter, in your own voice, breaks the sycophancy spell and is the technical mechanism behind the "What would change my mind" sections this book asks for.

[^sycophancy]: Sharma, Mrinank et al. (2023). "Towards Understanding Sycophancy in Language Models." Anthropic preprint.

## The Combined Test — fourteen items

`[verify — author to confirm canonical Combined Test items; the fourteen below are constructed from the five Cowork failure modes (Ch 7), Sommers's 1980 revision distinction, Wiggins & McTighe's Stage 3 assessment principles, and standard pedagogical quality criteria. The canonical list belongs in _lib_combined-test.md.]`

Fourteen items in four groups, reproduced as a scorecard in Appendix F. Applied in a twenty-minute sitting, not a three-hour one — the author at this point is fatigued, and a checklist that takes a full afternoon does not get used.

**Group A — Voice and specificity**

*Item 1 — Scene-first opening.* Does the chapter open inside a specific moment — a named person doing a named thing in a specified time and place? Pass: a reader can answer "where am I, who is here, what is happening" by the end of the first paragraph. Fail: the first paragraph could be the abstract of an article on the topic.

*Item 2 — Domain-specific examples throughout.* Are at least 80% of examples from the book's actual target domain? Pass: a peer in the field would recognize the examples as theirs. Fail: examples drift into adjacent domains the book is not about.

*Item 3 — Author voice consistent.* Read the opening and closing paragraphs aloud. Do they sound like the same person? Pass: yes. Fail: the closing has flattened toward magazine-article prose.

*Item 4 — No fabricated specificity.* Every percentage, every quoted study, every named report can be traced to a primary source. Pass: every number defensible. Fail: at least one "studies show" with no traceable source.

**Group B — Structure**

*Item 5 — Mechanism before terminology.* Concepts are explained from first principles before their disciplinary name is introduced. Pass: a non-expert can follow the explanation; the term arrives as a label for something already understood. Fail: the term arrives first and requires the reader to hold an unfilled box.

*Item 6 — Named trade-offs.* At least one place per chapter where a choice's cost is acknowledged. Pass: the reader sees the author thinking, not just concluding. Fail: the chapter recommends without admitting cost.

*Item 7 — No padded middle.* Every paragraph in pages two through four advances argument, delivers an example, or names a trade-off. Pass: no sentence whose absence would weaken the chapter. Fail: the middle contains restatement or connective tissue without load.

*Item 8 — Bridge question that bridges.* The chapter's closing question is answered by the next chapter's spec. Pass: you can name where in the next chapter the answer arrives. Fail: the question is decorative.

**Group C — Pedagogy**

*Item 9 — Capability statement honored.* The chapter delivers what the Blueprint.md capability statement promised. Pass: a reader who finishes can do the named thing. Fail: the chapter describes the topic but does not enable the action.

*Item 10 — Bloom's level achieved.* The chapter operates at the cognitive level its learning objectives claim. Pass: exercises at Apply level genuinely require application. Fail: the chapter says Evaluate and asks the reader to summarize.

*Item 11 — Exercises require the action they name.* Every exercise asks the reader to produce, identify, or evaluate — not to "think about" or "consider." Pass: each exercise produces an artifact or a documented decision. Fail: exercises are reflection prompts dressed as work.

**Group D — Authorial accountability**

*Item 12 — Author judgment present.* At least one paragraph per chapter contains a claim only this author could have made. Pass: a peer can point to the sentence and say "yes, that's [author]." Fail: every sentence could have been written by any competent commentator on the topic.

*Item 13 — Contested claims flagged or argued.* Where the field is split, the chapter either takes a position with reasoning or flags the dispute. Pass: the reader can tell where settled science ends and contested territory begins. Fail: the chapter assumes consensus where there is none.

*Item 14 — What would change my mind.* The chapter contains a paragraph acknowledging the kind of evidence that would falsify its main claim. Pass: the author has thought about how the chapter could be wrong, and said so. Fail: the chapter reads as if the author cannot imagine being wrong.

The two hardest to self-assess: items 3 and 8. Voice drift is gradual and you read your own chapter in your own voice even where the words are not yours. Nancy Sommers and Laura Saltz's longitudinal study of Harvard undergraduates found that the move from novice to expert was the move from rule-following to judgment.[^sommers-saltz] The test catches the failures the author can name. Voice, the irreducible thing, is not on the test. The author either supplies it or the chapter does not pass.

## Three passes — one chapter rewritten

The chapter being rewritten is `ai-for-designers/chapters/03-domain-research.md`. The Cowork draft was annotated in Chapter 7. Here are the three passes.

**Cowork draft, opening paragraph:**

> Domain research is one of the most important steps in any textbook writing project. Without good research, your book cannot succeed. In today's rapidly evolving design landscape, understanding what AI is doing to your profession is essential. Studies have shown that 78% of design firms are integrating AI tools into their workflows. This means designers must adapt quickly.

**First-pass rewrite (structure fixed, voice generic):**

> Before you can write a chapter, you have to know what is true in your field. Domain research is the step that produces this knowledge. The three-LLM prompt — run the same question through Claude, ChatGPT, and Gemini — is the method this book uses. Comparing three answers reveals where the field agrees, where it diverges, and where the model is making things up. The output is a research brief Blueprint will read at /i1.

What changed: the topic statement is gone; the fake number is gone; the structure of the method is named. What did not change: the voice is still generic. This paragraph could have been written by any competent technical author. Items 1, 2, 3, 12 still fail.

This is the version most authors stop at. It feels finished because the worst failures are fixed. It is not finished. Anne Lamott called this the trap — the productive feeling without productive output.[^lamott] The first-pass rewrite is improved enough to feel good and not improved enough to be yours. Stopping here is the polish trap: improvement at the level of words when the work that remains is at the level of voice.

[^lamott]: Lamott, Anne (1994). *Bird by Bird*. Pantheon.

**Second-pass rewrite (voice present, Combined Test passed):**

> Three LLMs answering the same question about your profession give you something no single LLM does: a map of agreement, divergence, and silence. Claude knows the published research. ChatGPT has read the trade press. Gemini has scraped the LinkedIn posts. Run the same prompt across all three on a Tuesday afternoon, and by Wednesday morning you have a domain research brief — the document Blueprint will demand at /i1. The three answers will not match. That is the point. Where they agree, the field has settled. Where they diverge, you have something to ask a colleague about. Where they all hedge, you have probably hit a contested claim worth flagging.

What changed from first to second pass: the opening is now a scene — Tuesday, Wednesday, three named LLMs; the mechanism is explained from first principles, with each LLM's training texture named; a trade-off is embedded — three answers won't match, and that is the point, not a problem; one judgment is present — "worth flagging" is the author's call, not the model's. Items 1, 3, 5, 6, 12 now pass. The paragraph is forty words longer than the first pass, but every word is doing work.

## The rewrite moves are teachable

Look again at what the second pass did, because none of it was talent. It converted *summary* into *scene* — "domain research is important" became a Tuesday afternoon and three named models — and that single move, summary to scene, is the most reliable way to make flat prose yours. It combined sentences for rhythm, letting a long clause build and a short one land, so the paragraph reads aloud the way you would actually say it. And it kept *voice* separate from *style*: style is the surface — word choice, sentence length, the things a model imitates easily — while voice is what only you carry in, the specific reads and judgments a model has never lived. A model can match your style in a sentence. It cannot supply your voice, because it was not there.

None of these are improvised. They are composition craft with a long pedagogy behind them — scene versus summary, sentence combining, the idiolect that makes your sentences yours. The companion volume *AI for Writing: A Practitioner's Guide* (Bear Brown) gives each a full chapter, including the move most useful at this seam: how to use an LLM to sound *more* like yourself, not less. If the rewrite is the one stage of this pipeline no prompt can do for you, that book is where the rewrite craft itself is taught. At a dollar, it is cheaper than the hour you would otherwise spend reinventing it.

## How many passes, and when to stop

Three passes is descriptive, not prescriptive. The right number is "until the Combined Test passes and the chapter sounds like you when read aloud." Some chapters need two passes; some need five. What does not vary is the set of conditions that signal done.

Three signals, all three required to stop.

First: the Combined Test passes. All fourteen items.

Second: you can read the chapter aloud without flinching. Voice drift is audible even when it is invisible on the page. A sentence that is not yours will catch in the throat. When it does, that sentence is not finished.

Third: a peer in your domain has read at least the opening and the closing. This is the lightest possible peer review and the single most effective use of twenty minutes of someone else's time. Items 3 and 8 cannot be reliably self-assessed. They need the outside ear.

There is a distinction the chapter has to be honest about: *finished* versus *avoided*. A chapter is finished when it passes the test and reads as yours. A chapter is avoided when you have stopped rewriting because you are tired and the chapter "is fine." The difference is not internal. It is external — a peer reader breaks the avoidance trap, as does reading aloud, as does the put-it-down-for-a-week test.

Advaith Bhat and colleagues at IUI 2023 documented that writers using LLM suggestions write faster but rate the output as less their own.[^bhat] The same pattern runs in revision: the chapter feels faster to leave alone than to rewrite. The chapter is finished when you have done the work that feels slow.

[^bhat]: Bhat, Advaith et al. (2023). "Interacting with Next-Phrase Suggestions: How Suggestion Systems Aid and Influence the Writing Process." *IUI 2023*.

One practical note on the blank-page counter-move: rewrite in your own document, not inline in the Cowork draft. Open a blank file. Type the rewritten paragraph from memory of the argument, not from copy-paste of the original. Mina Lee and colleagues at CHI 2022 measured convergence on shared phrasings even in writers who believed they were rewriting — the homogenization effect persists when the original text is visible.[^lee] The blank-page move breaks the pull.

[^lee]: Lee, Mina et al. (2022). "CoAuthor: Designing a Human-AI Collaborative Writing Dataset for NLP Research." *CHI 2022*.

## The seam, stated plainly

The pipeline is indifferent to whether the human rewrite happens. It does not check. The Finishing Pass in Chapter 9 will run on whatever text is in `chapters/`. The EPUB will validate. KDP will accept it. The book will be on sale.

The pipeline is indifferent. The reader is not. The reader who opens your chapter in six months will know in the second sentence whether this is your book or a Cowork dump with your name on it. Padmakumar and He's 2024 measurement of 10–20% lexical-diversity reduction in LLM-assisted writing is the empirical version of that reader's instinct.[^padmakumar]
<!-- FACT-CHECK FLAG: UNVERIFIED — see factchecks/08-the-human-rewrite-assertions.md --> The model's tendency is to converge on the cheap phrase forms that make up most of its training data. The rewrite is what breaks the convergence. Nothing else in the pipeline does.

[^padmakumar]: Padmakumar, Vishakh and He He (2024). "Does Writing with Language Models Reduce Content Diversity?" *ICLR 2024*.

Baldwin's claim is the principled version. The model has not lived your practice. It cannot produce text from that laboratory. It can produce text *about* what the laboratory probably looks like, based on the other texts it has read about other laboratories. The resulting prose is fluent, plausible, and not yours.

The seam is the gate where you decide whether the book is yours. The pipeline cannot enforce this gate. The author either does the work or the book is a Cowork dump with figures. There is no middle position.

## LLM Exercises

**Exercise 1 — Score the Combined Test**

Apply the Combined Test to one chapter from your `chapters/` directory. Score all fourteen items. For each, write the score (PASS / FAIL) and one sentence of evidence: the sentence in the draft that earned the pass, or the absence that earned the fail. Identify the three lowest-scoring items. These are your rewrite targets.

Then run this prompt on your three lowest-scoring items:

> "Here are three failures from a Combined Test evaluation of a Cowork-generated chapter draft: [paste the three items and their evidence sentences]. For each failure, name the specific rewrite move — not 'improve the voice' but the operational sentence-level action — that would turn the FAIL into a PASS."

Compare the model's rewrite moves to the five failure-mode responses described in this chapter. Does the model identify the same move, or a different one? When it differs, which is more specific?

**Exercise 2 — Three-pass rewrite of one opening**

Take the chapter you scored in Exercise 1. Rewrite its opening section — three to five paragraphs — three times.

Pass 1: Fix structure only. Remove fabricated specificity. Replace topic statements with mechanism descriptions. Do not attempt voice yet.

Pass 2: Inject voice. Scene-first. One author judgment. Domain-specific examples.

After Pass 2, run this prompt:

> "Here are two versions of a textbook chapter opening — a first-pass structural rewrite and a second-pass voice rewrite. Evaluate both against these four criteria: (1) does it open in a scene? (2) are the examples domain-specific? (3) is there one claim only this author could have made? (4) is any specificity fabricated or unverifiable? For each criterion, score both versions PASS or FAIL and explain the difference."

Use the evaluation to identify what the second pass changed that the model can name, and what it changed that the model cannot see.

**Exercise 3 — Stress-test Item 12**

Item 12 — author judgment present — is the hardest to fake and the hardest to teach. Take a paragraph from your rewrite and run this prompt:

> "Here is a paragraph from a textbook chapter. I want to know whether it contains 'author judgment' — a claim that could only have been made by someone who has lived the practice, not by a well-informed outsider. Evaluate the paragraph: does it contain such a claim? If yes, identify the sentence. If no, suggest one specific judgment this author could add — not an opinion about the field in general, but a judgment from practice that a peer in the field would recognize as this author's."

Read the model's response. If it identifies a genuine judgment, note what made the claim recognizable. If it suggests an addition, evaluate whether the suggestion is a real judgment or a placeholder shaped like a judgment.

**Exercise 4 — Build a complete revised chapter**

Produce a complete revised chapter passing all fourteen Combined Test items. For each item that failed in Exercise 1, write one sentence in a separate file noting how it was addressed in the revision — which paragraph, what changed.

Read the chapter aloud once. Note any flinches — sentences that catch in the throat. Address every flinch before continuing.

Put the chapter down for at least twenty-four hours. Reread. Revise any sentence that does not sound like you.

Then run the Combined Test again. If any item still fails, address it. The chapter is finished when all fourteen pass and the read-aloud produces no flinches.

## Still puzzling

What if the author genuinely cannot hear voice drift? Three counter-moves: read aloud (drift is audible even when invisible), wait a week and reread (distance restores the ear), have a peer read just the first and last paragraphs and ask whether the same person wrote both. The skill is trainable; the first chapter is the hardest because the author has not yet learned what to listen for.

What about voice drift in the rewrite itself? Lee and colleagues at CHI 2022 measured convergence on shared phrasings even in writers who believed they were rewriting. The blank-page counter-move breaks the homogenization spell: rewrite in a blank document, from memory of the argument, not from copy-paste of the Cowork draft.

Does the Combined Test apply to frontmatter and backmatter? Partially. Items 1, 2, 3, 12, 14 still apply. Items 5–11 apply differently — frontmatter does not need a worked example. Use judgment. The test is a scaffold, not a rulebook.

What if a chapter passes the test and the author still hates it? The test catches the failures it specifies. Persistent dissatisfaction is information. There is usually an item the test should have that this draft has not yet articulated. Investigate. Add it.
